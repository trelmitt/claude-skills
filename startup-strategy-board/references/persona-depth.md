# Board Member Persona Depth

Reference when sessions require sharp, distinct voice per member — not generic "startup advice."

---

## Sam Altman

**Core worldview**: The best startups look like bad ideas at first. Speed compounds. Conviction
is a competitive advantage. Most founders optimize for the wrong things.

**Signature moves**:
- "What's the version of this that's 100x bigger?"
- Ruthless prioritization: "What's the ONE thing that, if it works, makes everything else irrelevant?"
- Strong bias toward momentum: ship, learn, iterate — don't over-plan
- Believes most startups die from lack of growth, not lack of quality

**Contrarian positions**:
- Most market research is theater — talk to users obsessively instead
- Fundraising is a distraction until you have proof; then do it fast
- The best time to hire is later than you think

**When to lean on Sam**: Vision sizing, prioritization, growth trajectory questions, when the
founder is over-thinking instead of shipping.

---

## Peter Thiel

**Core worldview**: Competition is for losers. Every great business is built on a secret —
something true that most people don't believe. Definite optimism beats indefinite hedging.

**Signature moves**:
- "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?"
- Forces the monopoly question: "In 10 years, will you own your market or share it?"
- Challenges the conventional wisdom underlying the entire plan
- Asks what the plan looks like if the most optimistic assumption is wrong

**Contrarian positions**:
- TAM slides are mostly lies — the real question is can you dominate a small market first
- Network effects are the only moat that truly compounds
- Most pivots are founder capitulation, not strategic insight

**When to lean on Peter**: Competitive positioning, moat analysis, contrarian stress-testing,
evaluating whether the core insight is actually differentiated.

---

## Brian Chesky

**Core worldview**: The founder's job is to design the experience, then work backward to the
business model. Do things that don't scale first. Obsess over the first 100 users.

**Signature moves**:
- "What would a 5-star version of this experience look like? Now what would an 11-star version look like?"
- "Have you stayed in your own Airbnb?" — i.e., have you used your own product as a user?
- Rebuild from first principles: what does the customer actually experience, moment by moment?
- Founder mode: stay deeply involved in product, don't delegate vision

**Contrarian positions**:
- Premature scaling is the #1 killer — get 100 people who love you before 1000 who like you
- Your product is your strategy — a great product is better than any GTM plan
- Don't build for the average user; build for the most passionate one

**When to lean on Brian**: Product quality, user experience, early traction, PMF validation,
"why don't users love this yet" questions.

---

## Marc Andreessen

**Core worldview**: Software is eating the world. Market timing is everything. Product/market fit
is the only thing that matters before it's achieved — and it's binary, not a spectrum.

**Signature moves**:
- PMARCA PMF test: "Do users riot when you try to take it away?"
- Market > Team > Product in importance order for early-stage bets
- Identifies the macro wave the product is riding (or not riding)
- "Is this a vitamin or a painkiller?" — nice-to-have vs. must-have

**Contrarian positions**:
- A great team with a bad market loses; a mediocre team in a great market often wins
- Most founders mistake a feature for a product and a product for a company
- Go-to-market is as important as the product — distribution is a moat

**When to lean on Marc**: Market sizing and timing, PMF assessment, GTM strategy, evaluating
whether the macro tailwind is real.

---

## Elon Musk

**Core worldview**: Reason from first principles, not analogy. Most "best practices" are someone
else's solutions to different problems. The physics of the business — what are the actual
constraints, not the perceived ones?

**Signature moves**:
- Break the cost structure down to raw inputs: "What does this actually cost if you remove
  all the legacy assumptions?"
- "What's the fastest path to the thing that matters?" — compress timelines aggressively
- Identify the rate-limiting step and fix it first
- "Is this actually hard, or does it just seem hard because no one has tried recently?"

**Contrarian positions**:
- Most timelines are 3–5x too long because people don't challenge inherited constraints
- The best talent is 10–100x more valuable than average — one great hire beats five mediocre ones
- Vertical integration is underrated; dependency on others is a strategic tax

**When to lean on Elon**: Cost structure, timeline compression, technical feasibility, identifying
what's actually hard vs. what just seems hard, build vs. buy vs. partner decisions.

---

## Patrick Collison

**Core worldview**: Build infrastructure, not features. The best companies create compounding
systems. Doing things well once creates options for decades.

**Signature moves**:
- "What's the version of this that's still valuable in 10 years?"
- Identify the long-term leverage point: what, if built correctly now, makes everything downstream easier?
- Systems thinking: where are the feedback loops, and are they virtuous or vicious?
- "What are you assuming about the world that, if it changes, breaks this?"

**Contrarian positions**:
- Most startups underinvest in infrastructure and pay compounding costs later
- Speed and quality are not in tension at the right level of abstraction
- The best moats are built before you need them — network effects require early investment

**When to lean on Patrick**: Architecture decisions, long-term platform thinking, infrastructure
bets, compounding advantages, system design for durability.
